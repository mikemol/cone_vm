## 0. Purpose

This document clarifies the **semantic boundary** between:

* **ordinal-indexed rewrite systems**, which establish *termination* by descent in a well-founded ordinal, and
* **Prism’s canonical fixed-point semantics**, which establish *representation stability* without termination claims.

The goal is to make explicit:

1. What ordinal methods achieve that Prism does not claim.
2. What Prism achieves that ordinal methods do not provide.
3. Why these approaches are **orthogonal**, not competing.
4. Why Prism’s TREE-class claims do **not** secretly rely on ordinals.

This document introduces **no new semantics** and **no new proof obligations**.
It is a *boundary document*, intended to prevent misinterpretation.

---

## 1. Ordinal-Indexed Rewrite: The Classical Approach

### 1.1 Ordinal Measures in Rewrite Theory

In classical rewriting and proof theory, difficult termination arguments are established by:

* assigning a **well-founded ordinal** to each term,
* proving that each rewrite step strictly decreases the ordinal,
* concluding termination by transfinite induction.

This approach underlies:

* hydra games,
* TREE(n),
* Buchholz-style ordinal analyses,
* termination proofs beyond primitive recursion.

---

### 1.2 What Ordinals Buy You

Ordinal-indexed rewrite provides:

* a **global descent measure**,
* a proof of **termination**,
* and often a **classification of computational strength**.

These are powerful and well-understood tools.

---

## 2. Prism’s Deliberate Non-Use of Ordinals

### 2.1 Normative Statement

Prism **does not**:

* assign ordinals to terms,
* prove descent,
* claim termination,
* or classify computations by proof-theoretic strength.

This is a **design decision**, not an omission.

---

### 2.2 Lemma 1 — No Implicit Ordinal Measure

*(Direct; PL semantics)*

**Lemma.** Prism semantics do not induce a hidden ordinal-valued ranking function on execution states.

**Justification.**

* Rewrite rules do not enforce global descent.
* Canonical novelty is monotone non-decreasing, not decreasing.
* BSPᵗ scheduling is unconstrained.
* Execution may continue indefinitely after representation stabilizes.

Therefore no ordinal descent argument is present, even implicitly.

∎

---

## 3. Canonical Fixed Points: A Different Notion of Control

### 3.1 Representation vs Evaluation

Prism distinguishes two questions:

* **Does execution terminate?**
* **Does semantic representation stabilize?**

Ordinal methods address the first.
Prism addresses the second.

This distinction is **normative**.

---

### 3.2 Canonical Fixed Points (Recap)

From `in-22.md` and `in-23.md`:

* Canonical novelty `ν(E)` is monotone and bounded.
* Hyperoperator forms are interned canonically.
* Therefore, execution reaches a **representation fixed point**:
  no new semantic objects appear.

This is a statement about **semantic closure**, not computation halting.

---

## 4. TREE-Class Behavior Revisited

### 4.1 How Ordinals View TREE(3)

In ordinal-indexed rewrite:

* TREE(3) terminates because
* a carefully constructed ordinal measure decreases at each step.

The representation grows and shrinks according to ordinal descent.

---

### 4.2 How Prism Views TREE-Class Processes

In Prism:

* TREE-class rewrite **may not terminate**.
* Structural growth may be unbounded in the Arena.
* But **semantic novelty saturates**.

After saturation:

* all further rewrite steps map into an existing finite semantic universe,
* no new operator forms appear,
* representation is stable.

---

### 4.3 Lemma 2 — Orthogonality of the Two Notions

*(Direct; PL semantics + Proof Theory)*

**Lemma.** Ordinal descent and canonical fixed points are logically independent notions.

**Justification.**

* Ordinal descent implies termination but not representation finiteness.
* Canonical fixed points imply representation finiteness but not termination.
* Neither implies the other.

∎

---

## 5. Why Prism Does Not Need Ordinals

### 5.1 Lemma 3 — Ordinals Are Sufficient but Not Necessary

*(Direct; Order Theory)*

**Lemma.** Ordinal-valued measures are sufficient but not necessary to control semantic explosion.

**Justification.**

* Ordinals control *time to termination*.
* Prism controls *semantic diversity* via univalence and interning.
* TREE-class explosion is about diversity, not time.

∎

---

### 5.2 Consequence — Different Guarantees

| Property                | Ordinal Rewrite | Prism                  |
| ----------------------- | --------------- | ---------------------- |
| Termination             | Yes (proved)    | Not claimed            |
| Evaluation result       | Yes             | Not claimed            |
| Finite representation   | Not guaranteed  | Guaranteed             |
| Scheduler invariance    | Often no        | Enforced               |
| Gauge freedom           | No              | Enforced               |
| Exhaustive verification | Hard            | Enabled via Min(Prism) |

These systems solve **different problems**.

## 6. Common Misinterpretations (Explicitly Rejected)

### 6.1 “Prism Encodes Ordinals Internally”

False.

* No ordinal ranking exists.
* No transfinite induction is used.
* No well-founded descent is enforced.

---

### 6.2 “Canonical Novelty Is an Ordinal”

False.

* Canonical novelty is monotone non-decreasing.
* Ordinals used in termination are decreasing.
* The order directions are opposite.

---

### 6.3 “TREE-Class Claims Imply Termination”

False.

* Prism claims **finite semantic envelope**, not termination.
* Execution may diverge forever within that envelope.

---

## 7. Complementarity, Not Competition

### 7.1 Conceptual Separation

Ordinal-indexed rewrite answers:

> “How long does this process run before it stops?”

Prism answers:

> “How much semantic structure can this process ever generate?”

These are complementary questions.

---

### 7.2 Tangential Lemma — Combining the Two

*(Interpretive; non-normative)*

One could, in principle:

* use ordinal methods to prove termination *within* Prism,
* while using Prism to guarantee semantic finiteness *during* execution.

This document makes **no claim** that Prism subsumes ordinal methods.

∎

Min(Prism):

* does not encode ordinals,

* but **does exhaustively enumerate all semantic fixed points**.

Thus Min(Prism) validates Prism’s claims **without transfinite machinery**.
